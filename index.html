Three Decades of The Footy

Thirty-five seasons of the Australian Football League, 1990 to 2025, drawn from 6,872 matches across 43 venues and 18 clubs.

By Muaaz Khan (34445811) · Monash University, May 2026

01

Where the AFL lives

Despite the “national league” branding, the AFL still has a centre of gravity firmly on the south-east coast. Of the 18 clubs in the competition, ten share a single city. The chart below pairs two linked views: a choropleth of Australia shaded by the total wins won by clubs based in each state, and a horizontal bar chart breaking those totals down by individual club, coloured to match.

Top panel: each state is shaded by the total wins of its clubs since 1990 — Victoria is the obvious centre of gravity. Bottom panel: every club ranked by wins, colour matches the state above. Hover any state or bar for the full breakdown.

The competition was not always this spread out. The stacked area below shows where matches were played each season — a slow tilt out of Victoria, with new bands of orange (Queensland) and dark blue (NSW) appearing as clubs were granted into Brisbane (1987), Adelaide (1991), Fremantle (1995), Port Adelaide (1997), Gold Coast (2011) and Greater Western Sydney (2012).

Host state of every AFL match by season. The spike in Queensland in 2020 is the COVID hub: most Victorian clubs relocated to Brisbane for the back half of the season.

02

Champions of the modern era

Two trophies define an AFL season: the Premiership Cup for the winning club, and the Brownlow Medal for the player judged best and fairest. The lane chart below puts every premier on a timeline. Click a club in the legend to highlight its years of glory.

One coloured square per Grand Final win. Hawthorn’s three-peat (2013-15) and Brisbane’s (2001-03) stand out as the only true dynasties of the era; the most recent back-to-back belongs to Brisbane again, in 2024 and 2025.

Total premierships per club

Bar + dot strip: a faded bar shows the total count, and one coloured dot per premiership sits along the bar in chronological order. Hover any dot for the year and the result.

The Brownlow leaderboard — every medallist 1990 to 2025

All 40 medallists (including the 1996, 2003 and 2012 ties) ranked by votes received. Combined idiom: bar (length = votes) + colour (club) + text label. Patrick Cripps tops the chart with his record-breaking 45 votes in 2024; Matt Rowell's 2025 winning total of 39 is the second highest. Hover any bar for the full result.

The Grand Final scoreboard, 1990 - 2025

Two lines per season: the winning side in navy, the runner-up in maroon. The shaded band is the margin. Grand Finals have ranged from a 4-point Sydney heart-stopper (2005) to a 119-point Richmond demolition of GWS (2019).

03

Reading the ladder

The final ladder is a club’s annual report card. The grid below stacks 35 years of report cards side by side, one tile per club. The y axis is inverted: line near the top of a tile is a top-of-the-ladder season; line near the bottom is the wooden spoon. The gold-ringed dots mark premiership years.

Sustained excellence looks like Geelong’s slow plateau near the top. A rebuild looks like North Melbourne’s long sag after 2016. Newer clubs (Gold Coast, GWS) only start when they debuted, leaving blank space on the left.

Head-to-head, 1990 - 2025

For every pair of clubs (A, B), the cell is the number of times A beat B across all 35 seasons. Read a row to see who a club has dominated; read a column to see who has dominated them.

Diagonal is blank (a club never plays itself). The deepest blues belong to long-running rivalries: Geelong v Hawthorn, Collingwood v Carlton, Essendon v Richmond. New clubs (Gold Coast row and column) are still building a track record.

04

The travelling circus

An AFL season is a freight problem. Each non-Victorian club spends most of its “away” rounds flying east; each Victorian club takes annual trips west, north and across the Bass Strait. The map below shows how far each club actually flew in 2025, calculated by adding the great-circle distance from the club’s home city to every venue it played at that season.

Bigger and darker circles flew further. Fremantle and West Coast (both based in Perth) and the Gold Coast Suns lead the table, as expected. Victorian clubs sit on or near each other in Melbourne, where their bubble sizes overlap.

Does travel hurt performance?

One dot is one team-season. A long-flying season is plotted to the right; a top finish is up near the top. The dashed line is a linear fit across every team-season since 1990: the slope is gentle but real — more kilometres tends to coincide with a worse ladder finish.

The relationship is far from deterministic. West Coast’s 2018 flag and Brisbane’s 2024 flag are both heavy-travel team-seasons, sitting up the top of the chart.

How the game has changed

The AFL of 1990 looked different from the AFL of today: bigger scores, bigger blow-outs, fewer interstate trips. The two charts below track two of those shifts season by season.

Average winning margin

Home win rate

Margins have trended down, especially since 2017 as the AFL has equalised the talent pool through the draft and salary cap. Home advantage has also softened, though it still sits comfortably above 50%.

Every margin, every season

A strip plot of all 6,872 matches: each dot is a single game, vertical position is the winning margin. Reading the chart left to right, you can see the cloud of dots compress towards the bottom in recent seasons — fewer huge wins, more tight finishes.

Navy is a home-team win, red is an away-team win, gold is a draw. Hover for the matchup.
